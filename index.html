AI WORKFLOW DEMO

From raw input to reviewed marketing output.

A simple six-step workflow for using AI without removing human judgment.

1. Input

Collect raw marketing material such as campaign briefs, landing page screenshots, social post drafts, client notes, content calendar ideas, video concepts, or proposal outlines.

2. Identify

Clarify what type of marketing asset it is and what job it needs to do, such as awareness, education, conversion, proposal support, client update, or short-form video planning.

3. Diagnose

Review the asset for clarity, audience fit, hook strength, message structure, brand tone, call to action, production feasibility, and client objective alignment.

4. Fix

Turn the diagnosis into usable edits, stronger hooks, clearer captions, better proposal structure, alternative content angles, or cleaner briefs.

5. Review

Check the output before use, including false claims, tone fit, message clarity, client objective alignment, and whether the team can actually use it.

6. Confirm

Move the asset forward only after human review and quality control. AI supports the workflow, but the human keeps final judgment.

PRACTICAL USE CASES

Where this workflow helps marketing execution.

These are the areas where AI support can help the team move faster while keeping structure, review, and human judgment.

01

Campaign Idea Development

Turn one rough idea into several campaign angles, then review for audience fit, brand fit, and feasibility.

02

Content Repurposing

Convert one approved idea into short-form scripts, captions, carousel outlines, email copy, landing page sections, or proposal paragraphs.

03

Landing Page Review

Check whether a page section is clear, persuasive, and easy to act on, then turn vague feedback into specific copy edits.

04

Client Proposal Support

Structure messy inputs into clearer proposal sections while keeping strategy, claims, pricing, feasibility, and client fit under human review.

05

Singapore Customer Behaviour Localisation

Review whether wording feels natural for Singapore audiences, especially around tone, trust signals, and language that may feel too salesy.
